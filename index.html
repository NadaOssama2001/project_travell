Temple

Egyptian temples were built to worship the gods and to celebrate the pharaohs in ancient Egypt and in areas under Egyptian authority. The temples were seen as the house of the gods or kings to whom they were dedicated. Within these temples, Egyptians performed various religious rituals, which are the basic functions of the Egyptian religion, such as: offering offerings to the gods, reenacting their superstitious interactions during their celebrations, and banishing chaotic forces. These rituals were necessary to continue to preserve Maat, the divine order of the universe. The construction of temples and the care of the gods were the tasks of the pharaohs.

The Kings Festivities Road

The Rams Road is a road that is more than 3,500 years old, 2.7 miles long and about 250 feet wide, connecting the Karnak Temple and the Luxor Temple, and was discovered in the ancient city of Thebes (present-day Luxor), where sphinxes and ram's head statues are lined up on the sides. Date The construction of the Rams Road began during the New Kingdom and was completed during the reign of the ruler of the Thirtieth Dynasty Nakhtenbo I (380-362 BC), but the road was buried under layers of sand over the centuries.
